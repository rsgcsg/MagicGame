# Game Title: Arcane Workshop

**Genre Keywords:**  
Magic creation / shop simulation / RPG / procedural generation / engine-building / turn-based combat / card game / fantasy adventure / extraction-style exploration

## Game Overview

This is a world where magic and alchemy truly exist, but where their rules are still not fully understood. Countless unknown realms, materials, and magical laws are waiting for adventurers to explore.

The player takes on the role of an alchemy supply shop owner. They run their shop, research new magic, and use increasingly powerful spells to explore and conquer one realm after another, gradually uncovering the secrets of the world.

## Design Concept

### Core Gameplay

The game is mainly composed of three parts: shop management, magic exploration, and realm exploration. In the full version, these three parts form a loop:

**The player manages the shop to gain resources, uses those resources to explore magic, invents stronger spells through experimentation, uses stronger magic to explore and conquer deeper realms, discovers more unknown truths through realm exploration, increases the shop's reputation, and obtains rarer materials. The main story progresses gradually through this loop.**

### Shop Management

The player uses alchemical materials to craft magical goods and places them in the shop for sale. The player can decide product prices manually, but adventurers of different levels will decide whether to buy based on the shop's reputation. In the early game, the player can only rely on basic magic to maintain the business. As the shop's reputation increases, stronger adventurers will visit the shop, and the player will gain more money and more opportunities for experimentation.

### Magic Exploration

Magic exploration is the core gameplay of this game. The player uses different alchemical materials to draw or arrange magic circles. We want this system to be more than a simple fixed crafting table. Instead, it should be a generative system with fuzzy but discoverable rules. In other words, the game will not manually design every possible magic circle result. Instead, results will be generated in batches through an algorithm and kept within a reasonable range.

This magic system should make the player feel that magic is "mysterious but understandable." Through repeated experimentation, the player should gradually discover the relationships between magic circle structures, material properties, and magical effects, eventually learning how to create powerful magic.

Cards generated by the magic system should not be purely abstract numerical objects. They should produce effects that the player can actually feel. For example, the player should be able to defeat monsters with spells they created, combine different spells to achieve stronger results, and use specific spells against specific enemies. This strengthens the feedback and sense of accomplishment of magic creation.

The exploration and invention of magic contains two main parts: discovery and synthesis. Discovery means repeatedly experimenting to find the functions and uses of new basic magic circles. Synthesis means combining already discovered small magic circles into larger magic circles to achieve stronger and more complex functions. This system of combining small magic circles should contain some form of engine-building: the player needs to learn how combining different small circles can produce more powerful effects.

One of the main design goals of this game is to create a magic system that satisfies the conditions above and integrates naturally into combat.

### Realm Exploration

We want to develop a turn-based exploration and combat system that is tightly connected to the magic system described above. The player can bring the magic they have discovered into exploration and combat. This exploration and combat system should have replayability, present meaningful difficulty, and allow the player to gradually learn the rules of each realm through repeated play. The player then uses magic to conquer those realms. At the same time, the system should encourage the player to use many different types of magic. During realm exploration, the player may discover new magic circle forms, new materials, and new pieces of background story. After conquering a realm, the player gains reputation, increasing the shop's fame and income.

## Gameplay Design Details

This section discusses concrete implementation forms, gameplay details, and development feasibility.

### Basic Game Presentation

This section explains how game progression works, including how time and space are represented, how many scenes exist, and how scenes transition between each other.

**Time Design:**  
Game time advances continuously in real time. Three minutes of real time represent one in-game hour. 5:00–6:00 AM is early morning, 6:00 AM–5:00 PM is daytime, 5:00–6:00 PM is evening, and 6:00 PM–5:00 AM is night. The shop is open from 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM, and customers visit during this time to buy goods.

**Space Design:**  
The game has several basic scenes: Alchemy Shop, Magic Laboratory, Raw Material Shop, Realm Expedition Guild, Realm, and the corresponding Battle Scene.

The Alchemy Shop, Magic Laboratory, Town, Raw Material Shop, and Realm Expedition Guild are basically 2D hand-painted scenes plus clickable buttons.

```text
Raw Material Shop  <--->   Town   <---> Alchemy Shop (Main Scene)  <--->  Magic Laboratory
                         |
                         v
                  Realm Expedition Guild
                         |
                         v
                       Realm
                         |
                         v
                    Battle Scene
```

Scene transitions are handled through buttons. For example, after finishing experiments in the Magic Laboratory, the player clicks a button to return to the Alchemy Shop. After finishing a battle, the player clicks a button to return to the Realm.

### Alchemy Shop UI Design

The Alchemy Shop is the main scene of the game. The interface uses a 2D hand-painted or pixel-style background. On the left side of the scene, there is an exit door leading to the Town. Beside the door is a "Go to Town" interaction button, represented by a direction arrow and a town icon. On the right side of the scene, there is a door leading to the Magic Laboratory. Beside it is a "Go to Magic Laboratory" button, represented by a direction arrow and a laboratory icon. At the top of the interface, the shop reputation is displayed, such as reputation level, reputation progress bar, or star rating. In the center of the shop, there is a counter area used to display business information such as today's revenue, today's customer count, business hours, and order completion status. The shop also contains interactive shelves. When the player clicks a shelf, they can view the goods currently on sale, product stock, restocking status, and perform restocking actions.


### Town UI Design

The Town serves as a transition hub between several scenes, so the interface should keep clear scene navigation buttons. At the bottom of the scene, there is a "Return to Alchemy Shop" button, represented by an arrow and an alchemy shop icon. On the left side, there is a "Go to Raw Material Shop" button, represented by an arrow and a material shop icon. On the right side, there is a "Go to Realm Expedition Guild" button, represented by a right arrow, a guild emblem, or an adventurer guild symbol. The Town's main function is to connect the Alchemy Shop, Raw Material Shop, and Realm Expedition Guild, so the UI should emphasize clear directional guidance and location recognition.


### Raw Material Shop UI Design

The Raw Material Shop is where the player buys and sells magical materials. On the right side of the scene, there is a "Return to Town" button, represented by a return arrow and a town icon. In the center of the shop or behind the counter, there is an interactive shopkeeper NPC. When the player clicks the shopkeeper, a trade window appears. At the top of the trade window, there are three main buttons: "Buy", "Sell", and "Exit". When the player clicks "Buy", the window shows the list of magical materials currently sold by the Raw Material Shop, including material name, material icon, shop stock quantity, unit price, and the player's current owned quantity. When the player clicks "Sell", the window shows the list of magical materials currently owned by the player that can be sold, including material name, material icon, player-owned quantity, sale price, and selling actions. When the player clicks "Exit", the trade window closes and the player returns to the Raw Material Shop scene.


### Laboratory UI Design

The Laboratory is a functional scene where the player conducts magical research and crafts cards. The interface uses a coarse pixel or 2D hand-painted background. The scene contains stone walls, a wooden door, bookshelves, potion bottles, crystals, an alchemy desk, magic diagrams, and purple magical devices, creating a mysterious magical research atmosphere. At the top of the interface, the player's current resources are displayed, such as `Mana 28/60`, `Cards 18`, and a Settings button. At the lower left of the scene, there is a "Back to Shop" button, represented by a left arrow and text, used to return to the Alchemy Shop main scene. At the bottom center of the scene, there is an "Alchemy Table" interaction button, represented by a potion bottle or alchemy tool icon. When the player clicks it, the alchemy table window opens.


### Alchemy Table UI Design

The Alchemy Table window is the core crafting interface in the Laboratory. It is used to generate new magical cards through magic circles and materials. The window title is `Alchemy Table`, and the overall style uses parchment panels and purple title bars. The left side of the window is the magic circle template library, where the player can choose basic templates such as triangle circle, square circle, ring circle, and star circle. The center of the window is the magic circle editor, represented as a graph structure made of nodes and connecting lines. Empty nodes display `+`. The player can drag materials into nodes, and the interface can also represent nested magic circle structures. The right side of the window is the material bag, showing materials and their quantities, such as crystals, herbs, fire essence, rune scrolls, water orbs, and earth stones. At the bottom of the window, there is a `Generate Card` button. When the player clicks it, a card is generated based on the current magic circle structure and placed materials, then added to the `Card Backpack` area in the lower right.


### Realm Expedition Guild UI Design

The Realm Expedition Guild is a functional scene where the player views realm information, selects exploration targets, and enters realms. On the left side of the scene, there is a "Return to Town" button, represented by a left arrow and a town icon, used to return to the Town transition scene. In the center of the scene, there is an interactive front-desk NPC. When the player clicks the front-desk NPC, the "Realm Selection Window" appears.

The Realm Selection Window displays the list of currently explorable realms. The window can show each realm's name, icon, recommended level, danger level, main material outputs, possible enemies, and exploration status. When the player clicks a realm, they can view detailed information such as realm description, reward preview, resource cost, expected number of battles, and Boss information. At the bottom of the window, there are "Start Expedition" and "Back / Close" buttons. Clicking "Start Expedition" sends the player into the corresponding realm scene. Clicking "Back / Close" closes the Realm Selection Window and returns to the Realm Expedition Guild scene.

### Realm UI Design

The realm exploration interface uses a coarse pixel, dark cave-style UI. The overall structure is similar to the branching map in *Slay the Spire*. The center of the screen is the main realm map, made of multiple hexagonal or circular nodes. Nodes are connected by golden dotted lines, forming several explorable routes. The player enters the realm from the start node at the bottom, advances upward along connected paths, and eventually reaches the Boss node at the top. At each step, the player can only choose one of the next nodes adjacent to the current node, so the player must weigh combat risk, resource rewards, and rest opportunities when planning a route.

Realm nodes are divided into normal battles, mystery events, rest sites, and Boss rooms. Normal battle nodes use a sword or magic attack icon, representing entry into turn-based card combat. Mystery event nodes use a question mark icon, representing random events, clues, materials, or special choices. Rest site nodes use a campfire or crystal icon, where the player can choose to recover mana or spend mana and materials at a temporary alchemy table to craft new magical cards. Boss nodes use a skull or large red badge icon, representing the final challenge of the stage. A legend panel is placed in the lower-left corner of the map to explain the meaning of each node icon and help the player quickly understand the route structure.

The top of the interface displays the player's current exploration resources. On the left is the mana bar, showing current and maximum mana, for example `Mana 28 / 60`. Mana acts both as health and as an important resource for crafting during realm exploration. When mana falls to 0, the current exploration completely fails, and the player loses all cards, materials, and equipment brought or obtained during the run. In the top center, the current number of cards is displayed, for example `Cards 18`, reminding the player whether their available card resources are sufficient. Since many magical cards are one-time consumables, the number of cards is itself an important exploration resource. A Settings button is placed in the upper right to open the system menu or exit confirmation interface.

The realm map interface is not just a route selection screen. It also serves as a resource management reminder. Before choosing the next node, the player can judge whether to continue deeper based on the top mana bar, card count, and current route structure. If mana is low but there is a rest site ahead, the player may choose to take the risk and advance. If the number of cards is insufficient, the player may need to look for events or material nodes to replenish resources. If the player has already obtained enough rare materials, they can also choose to retreat at a stage checkpoint and bring the materials back to the workshop to create new magical cards and unlock new magic circle structures.

At the end of each complete stage, a stage choice window appears, asking the player whether to `Return to Workshop` or `Continue Deeper`. If the player chooses to return to the workshop, they can take back the materials, magic circle structure clues, and remaining resources they have obtained, using them for out-of-run growth. If the player chooses to continue deeper, they enter the next layer of the realm with higher risk and higher rewards. This design makes the Realm UI not just a map, but the core decision-making interface for risk control, resource consumption, and out-of-run progression.

The rest site interface uses an independent UI design. When the player enters a rest site, two large buttons are displayed in the center of the screen: `Rest` and `Craft Card`. The `Rest` button restores mana, helping the player extend exploration. The `Craft Card` button opens the temporary magic alchemy table inside the realm, where the player can use materials obtained during exploration and known recipes to craft new magical cards. Since crafting cards consumes mana, the player must choose between survival and replenishing combat tools. This interface should clearly show current mana, current card count, and the function of each option so the player can quickly decide their next strategy.

Visually, the Realm UI uses deep blue, purple, and warm yellow as its main colors. The background is a dark cave environment with stone pillars, hanging lanterns, purple crystals, and distant realm space, creating a dangerous but mysterious exploration atmosphere. UI panels use light parchment or beige backgrounds with dark coarse pixel outlines, keeping text and icons clear over the dark background. All buttons and nodes should use large shapes, low detail, and a coarse pixel style, making them quick to recreate and easy for the player to recognize.


### In-Battle UI Design

The battle interface uses a coarse pixel-style 2D card battle layout. The center area is reserved for enemy display, while the lower area is reserved for the player's cards and resource UI, preventing background details from interfering with core operations.

The battle UI is mainly distributed around the edges of the screen to preserve clarity in the central battle area. Slightly to the right of the gold count at the top is the equipment slot area, used to display the equipment currently carried by the player, such as weapons, armor, accessories, and relics. Each equipment slot uses an independent square icon frame with a clear border and dark background, allowing the player to quickly identify equipment types. The upper-left corner displays the gold amount using a gold icon and number, keeping the information simple and intuitive. A gear-shaped Settings button is placed in the upper-right corner to open the system menu or pause interface.

The enemy entity is placed in the center of the screen. At the current stage, the enemy does not need complex animation; a clear idle entity image is enough. The enemy displays its next-turn intent.

The player's main operation area is at the bottom of the screen. The player's current hand is displayed in the bottom center, with cards arranged in a slight fan shape to clearly define the hand area. Each card consists of four core parts. First is the card frame template, used to unify the card's overall structure and visual boundary. Second is the card image area, used to represent the card's magical effect or visual theme. Third is the lower text description area, used to display the card name, effect text, and necessary supplementary text. Fourth is the action cost icon in the upper-left corner, used to show how many action points are required to play the card. This structure allows all cards to share a unified layout; different cards only need to replace their image, text, cost, and color.

The draw pile is placed in the lower-left corner, represented by stacked card-back icons and displaying the number of cards remaining in the draw pile. The discard pile is placed in the lower-right corner, with a clear drop area that expresses the interaction logic of "drag cards here to discard." The discard pile is no longer placed in the lower-left corner; instead, it is separated from the draw pile spatially, reinforcing the distinction between "where cards are drawn from" and "where cards go after being discarded." The player can drag cards from their hand to the lower-right discard pile to discard them. This drag-based interaction is more intuitive than a separate discard button and better matches the feel of a card game.

The action points are displayed slightly above and to the right of the draw pile, while the mana bar is displayed near the bottom center close to the hand area. Action points are shown as a blue orb with text in the form of `3/3`, representing the player's remaining action resource this turn. The mana bar is designed as a continuous long blue bar with numbers inside. It does not use segmented orbs or complicated decoration, emphasizing that it is the player's core survival resource during exploration. Mana functions as both a traditional health value and a resource consumed by card crafting inside realms, so it needs to remain visually prominent, stable, and easy to read.

The `End Turn` button is placed in the lower-right area. It uses a large rectangular button style with a color clearly distinct from other UI elements, allowing the player to quickly end their turn. The current battle UI keeps only the core functions.

The overall UI style continues the unified visual language of the Alchemy Shop and Town interfaces: coarse pixel rendering, large block shapes, brown-gold borders, dark base panels, blue magical resource highlights, and parchment-style explanation layout. The goal of the interface is to clearly express the battle operation logic: the player draws cards from the draw pile, chooses cards from the hand area to play or drags them to the discard pile, consumes action points to perform actions, manages exploration risk through the mana bar, and clicks End Turn when appropriate. This design emphasizes the relationship between card operations, resource management, and exploration risk, making it suitable as the core battle UI for the current prototype version.


### Concrete Shop Management Form and Implementation

The player owns a magic shop, and the shop has a reputation value. The player can manually adjust the prices of goods for sale, then earn money over time based on the shop's attributes. Money can be used to purchase magical materials. Shop management is not a priority implementation target for the hackathon.

### Concrete Combat and Exploration Form and Implementation

Combat and realm exploration are similar to a *Slay the Spire*-style card game. The player brings some initial magical cards and materials into a realm map. The map consists of multiple nodes, including normal battles, mystery events, rest sites, and Boss rooms. Each time, the player chooses a route to explore. During exploration, the player obtains new materials and new magic circle structure clues. Then they bring these resources back to the workshop, create new magical cards, and continue challenging deeper realms.

Combat uses turn-based card battle rules. The player has an initial hand limit of five cards. They draw five cards at the start, and at the beginning of each later turn, if their hand is not full, cards are drawn from the draw pile until the hand reaches the limit. The player has action points, initially 3 points per turn. During a turn, the player can spend action points to play cards from the hand. In general, once a magical card is played, it is permanently consumed. Alternatively, the player can discard cards from the hand to the discard pile. Cards are not automatically discarded at the end of the turn. Enemies display their next-turn intent in advance, such as attack, defend, charge, summon, or apply abnormal status effects. When the draw pile is depleted, the discard pile is shuffled back into the draw pile.

The player has a mana bar. If the mana bar drops to 0, the current exploration completely fails, and the player loses all cards, materials, and equipment. Enemy attacks that are not blocked consume the player's mana.

At rest sites, the player is offered two options: rest to recover mana, or use the temporary magical alchemy table at the rest site to craft cards. The crafting recipes are the same as the recipes outside the realm. It is important to note that crafting cards inside a realm consumes mana, so the balance between mana and card count is a key part of the game.

The main difference from *Slay the Spire* is that this design places more emphasis on resource management, risk control, and out-of-run growth. Many of the cards created by the player are one-time consumables and disappear permanently after use. The player must obtain new materials during realm exploration. They can either use these materials to craft new magic circles inside the realm and continue exploring, or return directly, stopping while ahead. After each full realm stage, the player is given a choice to leave or continue deeper. Weighing whether to bring back more materials or craft more magic circles is an important decision the player must make.

### Concrete Magic Circle Form and Implementation

Magic circles are represented as graph structures in the game. Each magic circle consists of several nodes and the lines connecting those nodes. There is at most one line between any two nodes. The game provides several basic magic circle templates, such as triangle circle, square circle, ring circle, and star circle. The player can place magical materials on each node, or nest another magic circle inside a node. A magic circle with no nested magic circles is called a first-order magic circle. If a node contains a nested first-order magic circle, the current magic circle is called a second-order magic circle. If it contains a nested second-order magic circle, it is called a third-order magic circle, and so on. The action point cost of a magic circle increases with its complexity.

First-order magic circles are the foundation of the entire system. Each magical material consists of four elements: fire, water, earth, and wind. In the algorithm, a material is represented as a four-dimensional vector:

```text
[Fire, Water, Earth, Wind]
```

For example, a material biased toward fire can be represented as:

```text
[80, 0, 20, 0]
```

This means the material contains 80 points of fire element and 20 points of earth element. After the player places materials into magic circle nodes, each node obtains a four-dimensional elemental vector. The first calculation step of the magic circle is to let each node's elements propagate along connecting lines to adjacent nodes. During propagation, each node retains part of its own elements and distributes another part to adjacent nodes. The purpose is to make the magic circle structure truly affect the result: the same materials can produce different magical effects if placed on different nodes or connected to different neighbors.

After the first propagation, each node has both "self-retained elements" and "elements received from adjacent nodes." Next, the system combines these elements pairwise to form pair effects. Since there are four base elements, there are 4 × 4 = 16 possible pairs, such as:

```text
(Fire, Fire)
(Fire, Water)
(Water, Fire)
(Earth, Wind)
(Wind, Earth)
...
```

These pairs can be understood as the first reaction between two elements. However, not all pairs are kept as valid dimensions. For example, the system treats water and fire as mutually canceling, so `(Water, Fire)` and `(Fire, Water)` are not kept as independent dimensions. Wind and earth also cancel each other, so `(Wind, Earth)` and `(Earth, Wind)` are also not kept. To ensure conservation of total elemental amount, the coefficients of these canceled pairs are not simply deleted; instead, they are transferred to other valid dimensions. For example, coefficients produced by water-fire conflict can be distributed back to pure fire, pure water, or other designated dimensions.

At the same time, certain pairs are treated as equivalent. For example:

```text
(Fire, Earth) = (Earth, Fire)
(Fire, Wind) = (Wind, Fire)
(Water, Earth) = (Earth, Water)
(Water, Wind) = (Wind, Water)
```

After cancellation and merging, the original 16-dimensional pair vector is simplified into an 8-dimensional valid pair vector. The key point of this stage is that the system does not change the total amount of elements. It only changes the distribution of elements. In other words, the sum of all 8-dimensional vector coefficients across all nodes must equal the total sum of the initial four-dimensional elemental vectors of all materials.

Next, the system performs a second propagation. This time, the objects being propagated are no longer the four-dimensional base elements, but the newly generated 8-dimensional pair vectors. Each node again spreads its binary reaction vector along the edges of the magic circle to adjacent nodes. This process represents the continued diffusion of "initial elemental reactions" through the magic circle structure, producing more complex combinations with other nodes.

After the second propagation, each node combines its own 8-dimensional pair vector with the 8-dimensional pair vectors received from neighbors. Since two 8-dimensional vectors are combined pairwise, this produces an 8 × 8 = 64-dimensional quadruple vector. Quadruples can be understood as more complete magical structures, such as:

```text
(Fire, Fire, Fire, Fire)
(Fire, Fire, Fire, Earth)
(Water, Water, Water, Wind)
(Earth, Earth, Fire, Wind)
...
```

These quadruples are the basic source of final card effects.

However, the 64-dimensional quadruple vector must also be reduced. First, the system treats the order between two pairs as unimportant, for example:

```text
(WaterWater, EarthEarth) = (EarthEarth, WaterWater)
```

Therefore, duplicate dimensions of this kind are merged. Second, certain quadruples are considered unstable or mutually canceling structures and are not kept as final valid dimensions. For example:

```text
(Fire, Fire, Water, Water)
(Wind, Wind, Earth, Earth)
(Wind, Fire, Earth, Water)
(Wind, Water, Earth, Fire)
```

The coefficients of these dimensions also cannot be deleted directly. They must be transferred to other valid dimensions according to predefined rules. After merging and transferring, each node finally obtains a 32-dimensional valid quadruple vector.

Then, the system adds the 32-dimensional vectors of all nodes dimension by dimension, obtaining the final effect vector of the entire magic circle. This 32-dimensional effect vector is the core data used to generate cards. Each dimension represents a possible card effect, hidden effect, or waste effect. For example:

```text
(Fire, Fire, Fire, Fire) → Deal damage to an enemy
(Water, Water, Water, Water) → Gain action points
(Earth, Earth, Earth, Earth) → Gain block
(Wind, Wind, Wind, Wind) → Draw cards
(Fire, Fire, Fire, Earth) → Apply Strength to self
(Fire, Fire, Fire, Wind) → Apply Vulnerable to enemy
(Water, Water, Water, Earth) → Gain mana
(Earth, Earth, Fire, Wind) → Reduce enemy Strength
(Fire, Fire, Water, Earth) → Apply Strong to self
```

Some dimensions do not correspond to any actual effect. These are called waste dimensions. Even if a waste dimension reaches its threshold, it will not be displayed on the card. Waste dimensions mainly absorb unstable elemental combinations, preventing every elemental combination from becoming a useful effect. This makes magic circle results more random and exploratory.

Each valid dimension has a threshold. When the coefficient of a dimension reaches its threshold, that effect is visibly expressed on the card. For example:

```text
(Fire, Fire, Fire, Fire) corresponds to damage
Threshold = 20
Final coefficient = 89
Effect level = floor(89 / 20) = 4
```

If each damage level corresponds to 6 damage, then the card gains:

```text
Deal 24 damage
```

Similarly, if a dimension's coefficient is negative, the corresponding negative effect is triggered. For example, if the damage dimension is negative, the card heals the enemy instead of dealing damage. If the block dimension is negative, it reduces block instead of granting block. If the draw dimension is negative, it discards cards instead of drawing cards. This allows failed magic circles, conflicting material combinations, or unstable structures to produce costly results.

To prevent a card from becoming overly complex, each card can visibly express at most three effects. The system first calculates all dimensions that reach their thresholds, removes waste dimensions and hidden dimensions that cannot currently be displayed, then sorts the remaining effects by coefficient strength or priority, ultimately displaying at most three effects on the card.

Besides normal effects, some dimensions are hidden effects. Hidden effects do not directly appear as card text even if they reach the threshold in a first-order magic circle. For example:

```text
(Wind, Wind, Wind, Fire) → Generate a card
(Wind, Wind, Fire, Earth) → Affect multiple enemies
(Water, Water, Fire, Earth) → Affect multiple cards
```

These effects are only recorded in first-order magic circles and do not directly take effect. Their main purpose is to matter in higher-order magic circles.

The generation idea for higher-order magic circles is that the result of a lower-order magic circle can serve as input in a node of a higher-order magic circle. In other words, one node of a second-order magic circle can contain a complete first-order magic circle. The first-order magic circle is calculated independently first, producing its own 32-dimensional effect vector. Then the system projects this 32-dimensional effect vector back into a four-dimensional fire-water-earth-wind elemental input, which participates in the propagation and combination process of the parent magic circle.

In this way, higher-order magic circles do not simply add multiple card effects together. Instead, the results of lower-order magic circles continue to participate in new graph-structure calculations. A hidden tendency such as "generate a card," "multi-target," or "affect multiple cards" produced by a first-order magic circle may be activated in a second-order or third-order magic circle and become a true advanced effect. For example, a first-order magic circle may produce a hidden tendency to "affect multiple enemies," but this tendency is not displayed by itself. When it is embedded into a second-order magic circle and the parent magic circle generates damage or a negative status effect, this hidden tendency can upgrade the single-target effect into an area effect.

Therefore, the core idea of the entire magic generation system is: materials provide base elements, magic circle structure determines how elements propagate, elemental combinations determine effect dimensions, thresholds determine which effects are visibly expressed, and nested magic circles allow lower-order results to continue participating in higher-order combinations. In this way, the player is not just choosing materials. They are also researching structure, position, propagation paths, and nesting methods, gradually discovering more complex and powerful magic.


## Art Style

The overall visual style of the game combines coarse pixel art with 2D hand-painted fantasy alchemy. The art direction is warm, mysterious, and retro. The scenes heavily use wooden furniture, stone walls, parchment, potion bottles, crystals, magic books, runes, lanterns, and purple-blue magical light effects, creating the atmosphere of a "medieval alchemy workshop + magic laboratory + realm adventure." Main scenes such as the Alchemy Shop, Magic Laboratory, Raw Material Shop, and Realm Expedition Guild are presented as fixed 2D scene backgrounds, emphasizing spatial identity and functional zone separation. Realm and battle scenes lean more toward dark caves, ruins, magic node maps, and card battle interfaces, emphasizing exploration risk and resource management.

The UI style uses a unified design of parchment panels, dark coarse outlines, and purple-gold title bars. Buttons, windows, and status bars all use large shapes, low-detail chunky pixel icons, making them easy to recreate quickly and easy for the player to recognize. The main UI colors include beige, dark brown, purple, gold, blue, and small amounts of green and orange-red. The top status bar usually displays mana, card count, and a settings button. Scene transition buttons are placed at the bottom or on the left/right sides, such as Back to Shop, Go to Town, Go to Laboratory, and Enter Realm. Important interaction areas use large buttons and clear icons, such as the alchemy table, shelves, front-desk NPC, and realm entrance.

The game's core UI is based mainly on popup-style functional windows. When the player clicks an interactive button in a scene, the corresponding window appears over the background, such as the shelf window, realm selection window, alchemy table window, or magic circle crafting window. Window interiors are clearly structured, usually divided into a left list, middle editing area, right bag or information area, and bottom confirmation button. The magic circle crafting interface especially emphasizes graph-structure representation: nodes, connection lines, empty slots, material dragging, nested magic circles, and the generate-card process should all be displayed intuitively. The overall goal is for the player to understand the flow at a glance: "select template → place materials → generate card → add to backpack."

The realm exploration UI uses a branching-map structure similar to roguelike card games. The map is made of multiple nodes and paths, including normal battles, mystery events, rest sites, and Boss rooms. Nodes use simple icons to distinguish types, such as swords, question marks, campfires, and skulls.
